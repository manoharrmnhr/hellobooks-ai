# Bookkeeping

## What is Bookkeeping?
Bookkeeping is the systematic process of recording, organizing, and maintaining all financial transactions of a business. It forms the foundation of accounting and provides raw data needed for financial reporting.

## Core Principles

### Double-Entry Bookkeeping
Every transaction affects at least two accounts. The fundamental equation is:
Assets = Liabilities + Equity

- Debit (Dr): Left side of ledger. Increases assets and expenses; decreases liabilities, equity, revenue.
- Credit (Cr): Right side of ledger. Increases liabilities, equity, revenue; decreases assets and expenses.

### Single-Entry Bookkeeping
Simpler method used by small businesses where each transaction is recorded once.

## Key Bookkeeping Tasks
1. Recording transactions – Every purchase, sale, payment, and receipt.
2. Maintaining ledgers – General ledger contains all accounts and balances.
3. Reconciling accounts – Comparing internal records with bank statements monthly.
4. Managing payroll – Tracking wages, deductions, and tax submissions.
5. Tracking accounts payable and receivable.

## Chart of Accounts
- 1000–1999: Assets (Cash, Inventory, Equipment)
- 2000–2999: Liabilities (Loans, Credit Cards, Payroll Tax)
- 3000–3999: Equity (Owner Capital, Retained Earnings)
- 4000–4999: Revenue (Sales, Service Income)
- 5000–5999: Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
- 6000–6999: Expenses (Rent, Utilities, Salaries)

## Cash vs Accrual Accounting
Cash basis: Revenue/expenses recorded when cash is received or paid.
Accrual basis: Revenue recorded when earned, expenses when incurred. Required for GAAP compliance.

## Best Practices
- Reconcile bank accounts monthly
- Keep personal and business finances separate
- Maintain digital backups of all receipts
- Use consistent categorization across periods
