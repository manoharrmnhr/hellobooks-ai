# Cash Flow Statement

## What is a Cash Flow Statement?
The Cash Flow Statement (CFS) tracks the actual movement of cash into and out of a business. Unlike the P&L which uses accrual accounting, the CFS shows real liquidity.

Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, but cash is reality.

## Three Sections

### 1. Operating Activities (Core Business)
Cash generated from day-to-day operations.

Indirect Method (most common):
  Start with Net Profit
  Add: Depreciation (non-cash)
  Add/Less: Changes in working capital (AR, AP, Inventory)
  = Net Cash from Operations

### 2. Investing Activities (Long-term Assets)
- Purchase of machinery or property (outflow)
- Sale of equipment (inflow)
- Purchase of investments (outflow)
- Dividends received from investments (inflow)

### 3. Financing Activities (Capital Structure)
- Proceeds from issuing shares (inflow)
- Loan repayments (outflow)
- Dividend payments (outflow)
- Share buybacks (outflow)

## Free Cash Flow (FCF)
FCF = Operating Cash Flow minus Capital Expenditure (Capex)
FCF represents cash available after maintaining/growing assets.

## Key Cash Flow Metrics
- Operating Cash Flow Ratio = OCF / Current Liabilities
- Cash Flow Margin = OCF / Revenue x 100
- Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = (Inventory / COGS) x 365
- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = (AR / Revenue) x 365
- Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = (AP / COGS) x 365
- Cash Conversion Cycle = DIO + DSO minus DPO (lower is better)

## Warning Signs
- Persistent negative operating cash flow despite profitability
- OCF consistently lower than net income (earnings quality concern)
- Heavy reliance on financing to fund operations
- Rapidly increasing DSO
